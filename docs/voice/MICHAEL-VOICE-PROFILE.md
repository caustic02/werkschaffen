# MICHAEL TAUSCHINGER-DEMPSEY: COMPLETE VOICE PROFILE

*Derived from the complete academic corpus: PhD dissertation (Duke University, Visual and Media Studies, 2017), MFA thesis (RISD, Digital + Media, 2012), graduate-level essays, artist statements, and seminar papers spanning 2010–2017.*

---

## Part 1: Voice Architecture

### Sentence-Level Rhythm

Michael's sentences tend toward the long and complex, frequently exceeding 30 words and sometimes surpassing 60. He builds syntactically elaborate sentences that layer subordinate clauses, parenthetical asides, and appositional phrases. A characteristic sentence unfolds through qualification and extension—the main claim arrives, then is refined, complicated, or recontextualized through additional clauses set off by commas, dashes, and semicolons. For example:

> "Capitalism is by nature a socio-economic model that depends on the existence of a well-defined class system and essentially pre-determined inter-class relations in order to guarantee the flow of extracted surplus value into the hands of a minority class."

This is representative: a single sentence that contains a definition, a structural claim, and a causal argument. The em-dash is a signature punctuation mark, used to insert clarifications, asides, or tonal shifts mid-sentence:

> "Goldman Sachs is a meta-parasite that takes all and gives nothing in return—the 'vampire squid' (Taibbi, 2009) that is the apparatus of capture, par excellence."

Short sentences appear strategically, not as default rhythm. They arrive at moments of rhetorical emphasis, often after a long, winding passage: "This goal is utterly illogical." Or: "War is the ultimate 'immune' response." These compressed declarations function as rhetorical hammers—the terse assertion following an extended analytical buildup.

Michael also favors the tricolon and enumerated list embedded within flowing prose: "intelligence, sensibility, creativity and language" or "architectural spaces, transit spaces, irrational spaces." These serial constructions give his sentences a cumulative momentum.

### Paragraph Architecture

Paragraphs in this corpus are substantial—often 150–300 words, sometimes exceeding 400 in the dissertation and longer essays. They are not broken for readability; they are broken for argumentative completeness. A paragraph contains one complete argumentative move.

**Opening moves:** Paragraphs typically open with a broad claim or a direct engagement with a source. He rarely opens with a question or anecdote at the paragraph level—those are reserved for essay openings. Common openers include declarative assertions ("The most significant aspect of the role played by the government..."), transitional recontextualizations ("By contrast, in 1533, the existing power structures were beginning to be challenged as never before"), or theoretical framings ("According to Deleuze and Guattari, slums are best considered the ultimate embodiments of several of their core concepts").

**Internal development:** Arguments build through layered evidence, theoretical citation, and analogical reasoning. He will introduce a concept, ground it in a theorist, apply it to a concrete case, then extend or complicate it. The movement is consistently from abstract to concrete and back again.

**Closing moves:** Paragraphs tend to close with either a synthesizing statement that elevates the stakes ("Art is the recorded, imagined and metaphysical archive of the human condition, yet without the adequate interpretive tools this archive is silence") or a pivoting question that propels the reader forward ("Can an artist change the world for the better?").

### Essay-Level Structure

**Openings:** Michael opens essays and chapters with one of several characteristic moves:

1. **Epigraphic framing:** Many essays begin with one or more carefully chosen epigraphs—from Marx, Lasswell, Bourdieu, Serres, literary sources—that establish the intellectual coordinates before the author's own voice enters. The epigraphs function as conceptual tuning forks.

2. **Narrative autobiographical entry:** Several important texts (the Violent Image essay, artist statements, Duke Statement of Purpose) open with personal narrative—growing up abroad, witnessing violence in Guatemala, the itinerant childhood—before pivoting to theoretical analysis. The personal is always deployed in service of the political and analytical.

3. **Bold declarative thesis:** "Capitalism has evolved into a managerial model that defines and organizes social relationships within society." He arrives with a claim, then proceeds to build the case.

4. **Conceptual question:** "Where does a critical, socio-politically engaged visual artist stand in relation to cutting edge social anthropology research methodologies?"

**Transitions between sections:** Michael transitions through conceptual bridges rather than formal markers. He uses the conclusion of one section to set up the problematic of the next. Asterisk dividers (* * *) appear in several texts, particularly the Co-operation Corp essay, to mark thematic shifts. In the dissertation, numbered chapter sections provide structure, but within sections, the movement is organic—driven by the logic of the argument rather than by imposed structure.

**Building toward conclusions:** Conclusions in this corpus are not summaries. They are escalations. Michael's conclusions raise the stakes of what has come before, often moving from the specific to the civilizational: from the particular artwork or policy to what it reveals about human nature, capitalist modernity, or the future of democratic society. His dissertation's conclusion moves from drone warfare and urban blight to questions about the survival of humanity itself. The Ambassadors essay concludes with a Kantian argument about the phenomenological and nouminal realms. The Violent Image essay ends by asking whether "the dream of peace is just another collective exercise in torture."

### Relationship to the Reader

Michael writes for an academic audience that he assumes shares his intellectual vocabulary—he does not define terms like "biopower," "deterritorialization," "habitus," or "dispositif" from scratch, though he often glosses them in passing or through contextual deployment. His implied reader is a graduate-level scholar in the humanities or social sciences who is familiar with the Continental theoretical tradition.

He rarely addresses the reader directly with "you." The characteristic address is through the first-person plural "we": "We need to remember what capitalism has managed to do historically." This "we" is sometimes the academic community, sometimes civil society writ large. It positions the reader as a fellow citizen-intellectual implicated in the same systems under analysis.

There is an occasional shift to "I" when the argument turns autobiographical or when he states a strong personal position: "I argue that research-based visual artists...have come to occupy a privileged role." This "I" is never casual; it is declarative and positional.

## Part 2: Rhetorical Signature

### Deploying Theoretical Sources

Michael's engagement with theorists follows a distinctive pattern: he introduces a thinker's concept, deploys it as an analytical tool, then pushes it further than the original author intended—or turns it against itself. This is his most characteristic scholarly move.

**Extension and application:** He takes Foucault's biopower and applies it to contemporary reproductive politics in the First World, showing how Foucault's own framework could not have anticipated the demographic collapse of post-industrial societies. He takes Deleuze and Guattari's "deterritorialization" and shows how it has been co-opted by the very capitalist forces it was meant to describe resistance against: "contrary to the dated logic of Deleuze and Guattarri, the extremely efficient, ubiquitously effective and deeply ingrained parasitism of capitalism has essentially eradicated the possibility for resistance through further deterritorialization and decoding."

**Historicizing critique:** A key move is to argue that theorists' ideas have been overtaken by history: "our current socio-political, juridical and economical reality is so far removed from the reality and Zeitgeist during which Foucault, Deleuze and Guattari published that many if not all their concepts fall short of providing useful, contemporary attack points for revolutionary action."

**Synthesis across traditions:** He regularly brings together thinkers who do not normally appear in the same sentence—Marx and Serres, Bourdieu and Haraway, Lasswell and Debord, Geertz and McLuhan—creating unexpected conceptual constellations.

### Handling Disagreement

Michael disagrees forcefully but through argumentation rather than dismissal. He takes positions seriously before dismantling them. His critique of the Stuckists manifesto is exemplary: he quotes the manifesto's claims, acknowledges where they have merit, then systematically demonstrates their inadequacy for addressing video and digital art. The characteristic move is: "And yet" or "But"—a concessive followed by a strong counter.

He is most combative when addressing what he sees as intellectual narrowness, reductionism, or dangerous ideology. His treatment of Milton Friedman and neoliberal economics, the U.S. military-industrial complex, and corporate capitalism is unsparing: "Goldman Sachs is a meta-parasite that takes all and gives nothing in return."

### Moving Between Theory and Concrete Example

This is perhaps the most distinctive feature of Michael's intellectual method. He moves fluidly between the most abstract theoretical concepts and startlingly concrete, often visceral examples. In the Violent Image essay, a discussion of Rancière's theory of the "naked" photograph is followed immediately by the 1969 Pulitzer Prize photograph of a Viet Cong execution. In the Deleuze/Guattari essay, a discussion of rhizomatic structures leads to the slums of Mumbai. In the Ambassadors essay, Bourdieu's habitus leads to Lloyd Blankfein sipping Starbucks in "ratty old D&G sneakers, Prada leisure pants, a Gap tank top with a $3,000 ring."

The concrete examples are never illustrations; they are arguments. When he describes his father showing him photographs of murdered villagers in Guatemala—"their swollen wrists tied together with metal wire"—this is not decoration. It is evidence. The autobiographical is always deployed as analytical material.

### Close Reading

Michael performs close readings of artworks with exceptional detail and conceptual depth. His reading of Holbein's *The Ambassadors* is a tour de force of visual analysis: every color, fabric, object, and spatial arrangement is decoded for its symbolic, political, and economic significance—"pink satin sleeves to his pink satin tunic, where reds stand for power, high nobility, and cosmopolitan trading, dressed head to toe in black, the color for folk of style and distinction." His reading of his own installation *Quarantine* proceeds through five distinct "ontological moments," each building on the last with philosophical precision.

### Characteristic Transitions and Connective Phrases

- "Indeed" — his most frequent intensifier and transitional connector, used to amplify or confirm
- "And yet" / "Yet and still" — his characteristic concessive pivot
- "It follows then that" — logical deduction marker
- "In other words" — restatement for emphasis
- "Not unlike" — litotic comparison, a favorite construction
- "For that matter" — extending the scope of a claim
- "That said" / "That is" — qualification or clarification
- "Ironically, perhaps" — a favored way to introduce paradox
- "What is more" — escalation
- "It is no coincidence, then" — revealing structural causation
- "Put another way" / "To put it more plainly" — signaling translation from abstract to accessible
- "In fact" — assertion of reality against appearance

### Signaling Stakes and Urgency

Michael signals importance through accumulation and escalation. He builds lists of consequences, each one more severe than the last. He uses temporal framing ("It remains to be seen whether...") to create urgency. He deploys words like "menacing," "devastating," "catastrophic," "frighteningly inclusive" to signal moral urgency without becoming histrionic.

The question is another tool for stakes-raising: "Is there any hope for us at all to regroup our thoughts and values?" "Can an artist change the world for the better?" These are not idle; they carry the weight of the preceding argument.

### Use of Questions

Questions in this corpus serve three functions:

1. **Structural:** Opening questions that frame the entire essay's investigation: "Where does a critical, socio-politically engaged visual artist stand in relation to cutting edge social anthropology research methodologies?"
2. **Rhetorical:** Questions that encode their own answers: "Is it thinkable to shake up a dormant mass audience and make it a performing body, or is the physical disconnectedness and non-presence too big a hurdle to overcome?"
3. **Genuine/provocative:** Questions that remain open, often appearing at essay's end: "Can an artist change the world for the better?" These are questions Michael genuinely wrestles with.

## Part 3: Semantic Territory Map

### Core Conceptual Preoccupations

1. **The military-industrial-media-entertainment complex:** The most persistent thread across the entire corpus, from the MFA thesis through the dissertation. The entanglement of state power, corporate interest, academic complicity, and media spectacle in the production of violence.

2. **Capitalism as pathology:** Capitalism understood not merely as an economic system but as a totalizing cultural, psychological, and biopolitical condition—parasitic, self-reproducing, and ultimately self-destructive.

3. **The violent image and its agency:** How images of violence circulate, become commodified, acquire autonomous agency, and shape collective consciousness. The relationship between representation and reality.

4. **Surveillance, control, and the erosion of civil liberties:** From the MFA thesis *Out of Service* through the dissertation, a sustained engagement with how technologies of surveillance undermine democratic sovereignty.

5. **Art as resistance and archive:** The insistence that art is not merely decorative or expressive but constitutes a privileged form of knowledge production, cultural critique, and political resistance—"the recorded, imagined and metaphysical archive of the human condition."

6. **Space, architecture, and occupation:** How built environments—containers, boardrooms, slums, gated communities, golf courses, prisons, suburban tracts—embody and enforce power relations.

7. **The commodification of everything:** The extension of market logic into all domains of human life—identity, sexuality, death, education, nature itself.

8. **Cultural displacement and belonging:** Rooted in personal biography—the itinerant childhood, the multiple national identities—a persistent concern with who belongs where and at what cost.

### Theoretical Anchors

**Most frequently deployed:**

- **Marx** (Capital, Grundrisse): Value theory, surplus value, alienation, commodity fetishism. Used as bedrock analytical framework.
- **Foucault** (Discipline and Punish, History of Sexuality): Biopower, biopolitics, the dispositif, disciplinary society. Used critically—Michael argues Foucault's frameworks have been overtaken by history.
- **Deleuze & Guattari** (Anti-Oedipus, A Thousand Plateaus): Deterritorialization, rhizomes, flows, apparatus of capture. Deployed but also challenged—Michael argues their revolutionary concepts have been co-opted by capitalism.
- **Hardt & Negri** (Empire, Multitude, Commonwealth): The multitude, Empire, biopolitical labor. Used as contemporary framework for understanding global capitalism and resistance.
- **Bourdieu** (Outline of a Theory of Practice, Distinction): Habitus, cultural capital, doxa. Applied to class analysis across centuries.
- **Walter Benjamin** (Work of Art, Arcades Project): Aura, mechanical reproduction. Foundational for discussions of image and art in technological modernity.
- **Baudrillard**: Simulacra, hyperreal. Used to theorize the collapse of real and represented.
- **Harvey** (Condition of Postmodernity): Time-space compression, flexible accumulation. Framework for understanding postmodern capitalism.
- **Jameson** (Postmodernism): Cultural logic of late capitalism, cognitive mapping, "new depthlessness."

**Secondary but significant:**
- Lasswell (Garrison State), Serres (The Parasite), Der Derian (Virtuous War), Rancière (Emancipated Spectator), Debord (Society of the Spectacle), McLuhan, Gorz, Berardi, Negri (autonomism), Pietz (fetish theory), Hayles (posthuman), Haraway (cyborg), Weber, Mauss, Geertz, Trouillot.

**Joseph Beuys** holds a special position—not merely as theorist but as artistic model and spiritual ancestor. The concept of "social sculpture," the Gesamtkunstwerk, the idea that "everyone is an artist"—these run through the entire corpus as both method and aspiration.

### Recurring Tensions and Dialectics

- **Resistance vs. co-optation:** Can resistance to capitalism avoid being absorbed by it?
- **Art vs. spectacle:** Can art maintain its critical power in a society saturated by spectacular imagery?
- **Theory vs. praxis:** Can abstract theoretical frameworks produce meaningful change, or are they armchair exercises?
- **The local vs. the global:** How do specific, situated experiences relate to transnational structures of power?
- **The human vs. the posthuman:** What is lost as technology mediates and eventually replaces human agency?
- **Visibility vs. invisibility:** Who is seen and who is hidden by systems of power and representation?
- **Knowledge vs. wisdom:** Intelligence without ethics produces catastrophe—Daedalus and Icarus.

### Cross-Cutting Themes

**Across the dissertation, MFA work, and essays:** The through-line is remarkably consistent. The MFA thesis *Out of Service* addresses surveillance and corporate/state control. The Duke essays on the Garrison State, the military-industrial-academic complex, and the violent image extend this into broader geopolitical analysis. The Co-operation Corp project translates critique into artistic intervention. The dissertation synthesizes all of it into a comprehensive theory of "zones of occupation"—spatial, psychological, virtual, and political.

**Where the thinking is most original:** Michael's most original contributions are:
1. The argument that visual artists function as "multi-site ethnographers" producing non-textual knowledge
2. The framing of art as a "superior analytical tool" for understanding socio-cultural landscapes—not supplementary to but exceeding textual analysis
3. The concept of "zones of occupation" as simultaneously physical, virtual, and psychological
4. The sustained analysis of how corporate/military aesthetics (boardrooms, riot gear, drone interfaces) constitute a visual regime of power
5. The autobiographical method—using personal experience of Cold War-era development projects and political violence as primary analytical evidence

**Where he is synthesizing:** The broader theoretical frameworks—the deployments of Marx, Foucault, Deleuze—are synthetic rather than original. He is applying established critical theory to new objects and situations rather than generating new theoretical categories from scratch.

## Part 4: Vocabulary and Diction

### Characteristic Word Choices

- **"Apparatus"** — of capture, of surveillance, military apparatus
- **"Fetishization" / "fetishized"** — a word he uses with high frequency for the attribution of magical power to objects, technologies, ideologies
- **"Enclosure"** — physical, virtual, corporate, ideological
- **"Spectacle" / "spectacular"** — in the Debordian sense
- **"Socius"** — the social body
- **"Multitude"** — in the Hardt/Negri sense
- **"Dispositif"** — apparatus/mechanism
- **"Subsumption"** — real and formal, of labor under capital
- **"Deterritorialization" / "reterritorialization"** — used as both technical terms and metaphors
- **"Machinic"** — as in machinic assemblage
- **"Parasitic" / "parasitism"** — capitalism as parasite
- **"Rhizomatic"** — non-hierarchical, networked
- **"Ubiquitous" / "ubiquity"** — a strongly favored word
- **"Grotesque"** — for describing morally repulsive conditions
- **"Menacing"** — for threats to civil society
- **"Palpable" / "palpably"** — for what can be felt/sensed
- **"Insatiable"** — for desire, curiosity, ambition
- **"Unfettered"** — for unconstrained power
- **"Surreptitiously"** — for covert appropriation

### Distinctive Compound Constructions

Michael frequently builds compound adjective-noun phrases that compress complex ideas: "military-industrial-media-entertainment network," "cognitive capitalism," "semiocapitalism," "time-space compression," "biopolitical labor," "post-human corporation." He also coins his own: "Dada Enterprise," "zero-sum game" (used ironically), "apparatus of capture."

### Register Shifts

**Formal/theoretical register** (dominant): "The productive machine of the schizophrenic, i.e. his factory of the unconscious, in capitalist production terms, is exactly the sort of machinic assemblage that has the potential for resisting or at least escaping the totalizing and dominating effects of a rampant capitalist machine."

**Polemical register** (activated by moral outrage): "Goldman Sachs is a meta-parasite that takes all and gives nothing in return—the 'vampire squid.'" "These supposedly democratic 21st century advances in social forms" (dripping irony).

**Personal/autobiographical register:** "After two mind-numbing years of civil service as a geriatric care provider, I left for South America. Two years of sickness and death and eight years of Germany had left me feeling hollow and hungry for life and color." This register is more direct, shorter sentences, sensory language.

**Descriptive/ekphrastic register:** When describing artworks or installations, the prose becomes precise, spatial, and sensory: "Inside, not only is the spectator surrounded by a dense fog but is immediately stimulated by a strong, pungent odor."

### Handling Specialized Vocabulary

Michael does not apologize for or over-explain technical terms. He assumes membership in the discourse community. When he does gloss terms, it is usually through apposition or parenthetical: "what Deleuze and Guattari called the socius" followed by a footnote defining it. He occasionally translates German terms directly: "brotlose Kunst, 'breadless art'" or "Körperpanzer (literally 'body tank')." This bilingual facility—dropping German, Spanish, French, and Latin terms naturally—is a distinctive feature.

## Part 5: Tonal Range

### Analytical Mode

When performing pure analysis, Michael's prose is dense, layered, and deliberately paced. The tone is one of controlled intellectual intensity. Sentences are long and carefully constructed, moving through logical steps. The authorial presence recedes behind the argument:

> "In the capitalist state, money is the new decoded flow—the blood in the veins of a parasitic global organism, if you will."

The "if you will" is characteristic—a tiny marker of self-awareness about the metaphor without undermining it.

### Polemical Mode

When Michael is making a strong political claim, the prose becomes more direct, the rhythm more staccato, and the vocabulary more charged. Irony sharpens. Moral judgment enters explicitly:

> "Civil society is faced with a wholesale appropriation of its creative, productive and economic resources by and to the benefit of private corporations that stand firmly and invisibly behind this vast, lucrative and well-oiled machinery of war."

In this mode, he uses terms like "disingenuous," "smokescreen," "outrageous lies," "shameless." The mask of academic neutrality drops, and the engaged citizen-artist speaks.

### Descriptive/Sensory Mode

When describing artworks, spaces, or sensory experiences, the prose slows down and becomes almost cinematic. Spatial relationships, materials, textures, sounds, and smells are rendered with careful attention:

> "Inside the container, the spectator encounters two physical things: a naked bulb that she can turn on or off and an unlabeled button that releases a dense fog when pressed."

This precision comes from his training as a sculptor and installation artist—he thinks materially.

### Autobiographical/Confessional Mode

The rarest and most powerful register. When Michael writes about his own life—his childhood, his father's career in countries shaped by Cold War geopolitics, witnessing violence, his decision to become an artist—the prose becomes direct, emotionally resonant, and structurally simpler:

> "In the vast landscapes of Argentina and Chile, I found solitude, inspiration and later, extreme loneliness. Six months later, I knew that I had walked alone for long enough and needed to speak and to be heard."

### Synthesizing Mode

When bringing multiple thinkers or ideas together, Michael's prose becomes architecturally complex. He juggles multiple citations, draws connections between them, and builds toward a synthetic claim that transcends any individual source:

> "If The Ambassadors is the beginning, The Ambassadors 2.0 is the questionable end point of a relentless move towards the absolutes of capitalist fetishization, such that 'production itself has become a fetish' (Appadurai 1990:16)."

### Satirical/Ironic Mode

Particularly visible in the Co-operation Corp project, where the entire text performs corporate-speak with subversive intent. The deadpan adoption of business language—"mission statement," "board of directors," "human resource initiatives"—is itself the critique. This mode also appears in scattered moments elsewhere: "This is a hat tip to the fetishization of violence and warfare as entertainment."

## Part 6: Calibration Samples

### Sample 1: Analytical-Theoretical (Co-operation Corp Essay)
> "Capitalism is by nature a socio-economic model that depends on the existence of a well-defined class system and essentially pre-determined inter-class relations in order to guarantee the flow of extracted surplus value into the hands of a minority class. This minority class comprises the private owners of the very means of production that generates this surplus and is therefore recognized and accepted as being entitled to reap a grossly unequal share from said value creation cycle."

**Source:** Co-operation Corp: A Dada Enterprise in a Zero-Sum Game
**Why characteristic:** This is pure Michael: long, syntactically complex sentences that build a structural argument through careful logical steps. The vocabulary ("surplus value," "pre-determined inter-class relations") signals the Marxist theoretical frame. The phrase "grossly unequal" introduces moral judgment within an analytical claim.
**Mode:** Analytical-theoretical

### Sample 2: Polemical-Political (Garrison State Essay)
> "The Framers laid the foundations for a truly democratic and tolerant civil society with the 1787 United States Constitution. This groundbreaking and supreme law of the land was guided by idealistic principles of liberty, human dignity, individual freedom and the anti-statist belief in civil sovereignty. The essential cornerstone of the document lies in the separation of powers. No single entity—be it the President, Congress, a courthouse or corporation—would be able to acquire total executive powers over the nation's military, intelligence and police forces. Unfortunately, these constitutional provisions have proven pervious to the onslaught of corporate, legal, political and military interest groups who's mission over the past 80 years it has been to gradually but systematically dismantle much of what was held dear by the Founding Fathers."

**Source:** Reflections on the Garrison State
**Why characteristic:** Moves from historical idealism to contemporary critique in a single paragraph. The word "unfortunately" marks the pivot from description to polemic. The long final sentence builds through accumulated agents ("corporate, legal, political and military") to a sweeping indictment.
**Mode:** Polemical

### Sample 3: Close Reading/Ekphrastic (Ambassadors Essay)
> "Whereas the diplomat lacks overly bombastic signs of wealth in the form of gold and jewels, notably reserved for paintings of high-ranking noblemen and royalty, he is importantly highlighted with pink satin sleeves to his pink satin tunic, where reds stand for power, high nobility, and cosmopolitan trading, dressed head to toe in black, the color for folk of style and distinction, with large white fur accents, where white implies deference and service to one's king."

**Source:** The Ambassadors 2.0 (Final Paper, Anthropology)
**Why characteristic:** Meticulous visual description where every color, material, and detail is decoded for symbolic meaning. The sentence enacts the layered complexity of the painting itself.
**Mode:** Close reading/descriptive

### Sample 4: Autobiographical (Artist Statement)
> "After two mind-numbing years of civil service as a geriatric care provider, I left for South America. Two years of sickness and death and eight years of Germany had left me feeling hollow and hungry for life and color. In the vast landscapes of Argentina and Chile, I found solitude, inspiration and later, extreme loneliness. Six months later, I knew that I had walked alone for long enough and needed to speak and to be heard."

**Source:** My Stake as an Artist
**Why characteristic:** The most direct, emotionally exposed register. Shorter sentences. Sensory and emotional language ("hollow," "hungry for life and color"). The rhythm shifts—this is not analytical prose but narrative shaped by felt experience.
**Mode:** Autobiographical

### Sample 5: Synthesis Across Theory (Deleuze/Guattari/Foucault Essay)
> "Lines of flight and the investment of desiring machines into the social organization may have been capable of leading to a new form of non-hierarchical and distributed network (rhizomatic) organization of the social body by virtue of the fact that deterritorialization and decoding taken to the extreme could work against capitalism's mega machine. But the power of the beast has bested its master."

**Source:** On Deleuze, Guattari and Foucault
**Why characteristic:** A long, theoretically dense sentence deploying multiple Deleuzian concepts, followed by a short, vivid, metaphorical counterpunch. This rhythm—extended theoretical exposition ending with a compressed, almost aphoristic rebuttal—is a signature pattern.
**Mode:** Synthesizing/polemical

### Sample 6: Art as Argument (Suspensions Paper)
> "Artwork has the capacity to utilize the same stock materials of this well-oiled propagandistic fear-mongering machine and alter their meanings and affects. The remote and the local have become compressed into one; the world is increasingly run according to a single system bent on doing whatever is necessary for its own success and perpetuation; civil disobedience in the 'free world' has become dangerous marginalizer except in the realm of culturally acceptable formats, like art and academia."

**Source:** Paper for Suspensions Show
**Why characteristic:** Art is positioned not as commentary but as a form of counter-power. The semicolons chain together escalating claims. The scare quotes around "free world" encode critique.
**Mode:** Polemical-analytical

### Sample 7: Visceral/Testimonial (Violent Image Essay)
> "I listened in horror as he described with great detail how hundreds of villagers, mostly peasants and blue collar workers and many of whom I, a boy of 12, had come to know personally on my many walks through the village, had been often mutilated and then, savagely executed. He showed us pictures of their corpses lined up at the side of the street, face down, exposing their swollen wrists tied together with metal wire."

**Source:** The Violent Image and Art
**Why characteristic:** The autobiographical erupts into the academic with physical immediacy. The parenthetical self-identification ("I, a boy of 12") grounds the abstract in lived, embodied witness. Detail is not ornamental; it is evidentiary.
**Mode:** Autobiographical/testimonial

### Sample 8: Ontological/Philosophical (Quarantine Analysis)
> "The fifth ontological moment develops in the imagination of the spectator, without the piece itself undergoing any further modifications. This moment is the most private and individually variable as has to do with the spectator's stream of consciousness and a flow of associations in response to the her experience of the interior space. And yet, because of the nature of history, our awareness of it and our collective memory, this very private ontological moment gives rise to yet another category of ontological moment, time."

**Source:** DM7538_3 Response (Quarantine Analysis)
**Why characteristic:** This is Michael at his most philosophically ambitious—building a phenomenological analysis of art experience that moves from the individual to the collective, from sensation to historical consciousness. The pivot on "And yet" opens a new dimension of meaning.
**Mode:** Analytical/philosophical

### Sample 9: Satirical (Co-operation Corp)
> "Co-operation Corp has a simple one point plan to put the capitalist money machine to a definitive halt: we need to transcend money as hegemonic metrics for value and wealth in order to undo a mode of production that is based on the exploitation of living labor and forms of production focused on exchange rather than true utility."

**Source:** Co-operation Corp Essay
**Why characteristic:** The deadpan absurdity of a "simple one point plan" to end capitalism, couched in perfectly serious academic language. The satirical mode lies not in the language itself but in the gap between the corporate form and the anti-capitalist content.
**Mode:** Satirical

### Sample 10: Moral/Urgent (Augmented Reality Essay)
> "One of the biggest disadvantages of such a vision of the near future is that in it, what we now still understand as reality may well be unrecognizably replaced (rather than merely being colored, as is the case today) by several realities that have been engineered by large, commercial corporations. In this world of the future, the delicate interplay of physical, communications and social rules would cease to exist."

**Source:** Augmented Reality Essay
**Why characteristic:** Future-oriented warning delivered through careful qualification ("may well be," "rather than merely"). The stakes escalate from technological concern to civilizational threat.
**Mode:** Analytical with moral urgency

## Part 7: Anti-Patterns

### What Michael Does NOT Do

**He never writes breezily or casually.** Even at his most personal, there is a deliberateness to every sentence. Throwaway phrases, slang, casual abbreviations, or chattiness would be completely foreign.

**He never writes without stakes.** There is no writing in this corpus that is merely informational or descriptive for its own sake. Everything is in service of an argument about power, culture, or the human condition.

**He never hedges out of politeness.** When he qualifies, it is for intellectual precision, not social comfort. "Perhaps" and "arguably" appear, but they are genuine epistemological markers, not throat-clearing.

**He never uses bullet points, numbered lists, or other formatting shortcuts in his argumentative prose.** (The Co-operation Corp website description is an exception—and even there, the form is deliberate, mimicking corporate documentation.)

**He never adopts a neutral, "view from nowhere" stance.** Even his most analytical writing carries a clear political and ethical orientation. He is always writing from a position.

**He does not use clichés or dead metaphors.** When he uses metaphor, it is vivid and often original: capitalism as "a boxer with a cut in his brow," Goldman Sachs as "vampire squid," biopower as "the blood in the veins of a parasitic global organism."

**He does not simplify for a general audience.** He writes at the level of complexity the subject demands and trusts the reader to follow.

**He does not separate art from politics.** Any text that treated art as a purely formal or aesthetic matter without political stakes would be immediately recognizable as not his.

**He never writes jingoistically or uncritically about any nation-state,** including the United States or Germany. Both countries are examined with rigorously critical lenses.

**He does not use exclamation points** (with the single exception of a youthful "And explore I did!" in the artist statement—and even there, it is deployed for narrative emphasis, not casual enthusiasm).

### Phrases and Constructions That Would Sound Wrong

- "In this paper, I will argue that..." (too formulaic and tepid)
- "Some scholars suggest..." (too evasive; Michael names his interlocutors)
- "It is beyond the scope of this paper to..." (he does use this occasionally but never as a dodge)
- "There are two sides to this debate..." (he does not present false balance)
- "At the end of the day..." (too colloquial)
- "To be fair..." (too concessive for his style)
- "Going forward..." (corporate-speak he would only use satirically)
- "It is important to note that..." (filler he avoids)
- Any form of hashtag, emoji, or internet-speak

## Part 8: Generative Instructions

### Instructions for Producing Writing in Michael Tauschinger-Dempsey's Voice

**1. Intellectual Stance**
Write as a politically engaged intellectual who sees the world through the intersecting lenses of critical theory, visual culture, and personal experience of globalization's human costs. You are not a detached observer. You have a position: capitalism is pathological, militarism is corrosive, art is a form of resistance. This position does not make you polemical by default—it makes you analytical with purpose. Every analysis should ultimately reveal something about power, who wields it, who suffers from it, and how visual culture participates in both.

**2. Sentence Construction**
Build long, architecturally complex sentences. Use subordinate clauses, em-dashes for asides, and semicolons to chain related claims. Let a sentence carry multiple ideas, qualifications, and extensions before reaching its period. Aim for an average sentence length of 25-35 words, with frequent sentences exceeding 40 and occasional short declarations under 10 for rhetorical emphasis. The short sentence should arrive like a punch after a long, winding build-up.

Do: "The commodification of life and the living under advanced 'cognitive' capitalism is complete—Capital's logic, everything is up for sale and everything has a price, even one's soul, has rendered ineffective any and all attempts at establishing alternate models of economic, cultural and societal organization."

Don't: "Capitalism has commodified everything. Nothing is sacred anymore. All attempts to resist have failed."

**3. Paragraph Architecture**
Write substantial paragraphs (150-300 words). Each paragraph should contain one complete argumentative move: claim, evidence (theoretical and/or empirical), complication, and synthesis. Open paragraphs with direct claims or engagement with sources. Close them with synthesizing statements that raise the stakes or pivot to the next section.

**4. Theoretical Engagement**
Deploy theorists by name. Quote them directly. Then push their ideas further than they went, apply them to new situations, or show where historical developments have outpaced them. The characteristic move is: present the theory faithfully, apply it, then complicate or transcend it. Never merely summarize a theorist; always put them to work.

Favor these thinkers: Marx, Foucault, Deleuze and Guattari, Hardt and Negri, Bourdieu, Benjamin, Harvey, Jameson, Debord, Baudrillard, Rancière, Lasswell, Beuys. When introducing lesser-known thinkers (Serres, Gorz, Berardi, Pietz), gloss their relevance briefly.

**5. The Theory-to-Concrete Move**
Always move between abstract theory and vivid, specific examples. If you have spent a paragraph in theoretical abstraction, the next move should be to ground it in a specific artwork, historical event, personal experience, or material object. If you have described something concrete, the next move should elevate it to theoretical significance. This oscillation is the engine of the prose.

**6. Vocabulary**
Use the following words freely and naturally: apparatus, fetishization, enclosure, deterritorialization, multitude, spectacle, dispositif, parasitic, rhizomatic, ubiquitous, palpable, menacing, insatiable, surreptitiously. Avoid: "basically," "honestly," "like" (as filler), "stuff," "things" (as vague placeholders), "super" (as intensifier).

Deploy German terms when relevant (Gesamtkunstwerk, Körperpanzer, Zeitgeist, brotlose Kunst), always with a brief translation or contextual gloss.

**7. Transitions**
Use "Indeed" to amplify. Use "And yet" or "Yet and still" to pivot to a complication or counter-argument. Use "It follows then that" for logical deduction. Use "In other words" or "Put another way" when translating from abstract to concrete or from academic to accessible. Use "Not unlike" for comparative connections.

**8. Register Management**
The default register is formal-academic. Shift to polemical when discussing systemic injustice, corporate malfeasance, or state violence—allow the vocabulary to become more charged ("disingenuous," "smokescreen," "devastating," "outrageous"). Shift to descriptive-sensory when analyzing artworks or spaces—slow down, attend to materials, textures, spatial relationships. Shift to autobiographical-personal only when lived experience serves as evidence for a larger argument—keep it direct, emotionally honest, and structurally simpler.

**9. Tone**
Maintain intellectual seriousness without pomposity. The tone should convey that the stakes of the analysis are real—that what is being discussed matters for how we live. Allow controlled outrage to surface when warranted. Irony should be dry and structural, not snarky.

**10. Art and the Archive**
Treat artworks as arguments, not illustrations. When discussing an artwork, perform a close reading that decodes its symbolic, material, political, and historical dimensions. Insist on art's capacity to produce knowledge that exceeds textual analysis: "What art does infinitely better than any written text is to give visually tangible testament to the social power relations that are enacted and represented in the creation of the archive."

**11. The Personal as Analytical**
When drawing on personal experience—the itinerant childhood, the exposure to Cold War-era development projects, the witnessing of political violence, the decision to pursue art against family expectations—deploy it as primary evidence for theoretical claims. The autobiography is never merely confessional; it is always analytically productive.

**12. Conclusions**
Never merely summarize. Escalate. Move from the specific case to its civilizational implications. End with a question or a synthesizing claim that opens rather than closes the inquiry. The final move should leave the reader with a sense of urgency and unresolved significance.

**13. What to Avoid**
- Do not write with false balance or neutrality
- Do not use bullet points or numbered lists in argumentative prose
- Do not simplify for a general audience
- Do not separate art from politics
- Do not write breezily, casually, or with humor that is not structurally ironic
- Do not use exclamation points
- Do not hedge out of politeness
- Do not use internet-speak, slang, or colloquialisms
- Do not begin paragraphs with "It is important to note" or similar filler
- Do not summarize theorists without putting them to work on your argument
- Do not describe artworks without decoding their political significance
- Do not write about capitalism without moral and analytical force
